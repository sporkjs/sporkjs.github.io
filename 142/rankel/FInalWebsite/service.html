Service Dogs

Please keep in mind that not all disabilities are visible and those that are aren't any easier. Service dogs are there to help lessen the impact of the handlers disability.

Service dogs aren't limited to just hearing and guide dogs. Dogs are amazing animals and can be trained to do a number of things. These catagories can sometimes be overlapped. For example, a handler may classify their dog as a wheelchair assistant and seizure alert dog. This doesn't mean it's anyless of a service dog, the dog just covers a wider variety of tasks.

Under the ADA law, service dogs do not need to be registered. They are also protected by the Fair Housing Act (FHA) meaning a service dog cannot be denied to live in an apartment or a no animal rental house.

Service dogs are trained to do a specific set of tasks for their handler. This can include a number of commands including basic obedience. Some handlers will self-train or have a professional help them train their dog. Here are a few commands that a service dog might perform for their handler:

  • Momentum Pull / Brace command
  • Deep Pressure Therapy
  • Interupt and re-direct an action
  • Bring help
  • Retrieve medication
  • Pick up items

Again, tasks are based upon what best fits the handlers needs. Some service dogs don't need to be trained certain tasks because their owner won't need that specific kind of help. There are many things a service dog can do and in the business, you either use it or lose it.

Types of Service Dogs
Psychiatric Service Dog Autism Assistance Dogs Wheelchair Assistance Dogs Diabetic Alert Dogs Seizure Alert Dogs
Assists their handler with a psychiatric disability such as anxiety, depression or PTSD via specific, trained tasks. The handler can vary widely in presentation but often appears to not have a disability. Often cited as having an "invisible" disability. Assists in calming and grounding an individual on the autism spectrum via tactile or deep pressure stimulation. May also assist in teaching life skills, maintaining boundaries or finding a "runner". Handler is likely to be a child, but could be older. They may or may not show visible signs of disability, and may or may not be verbal. To assist their partner by retrieving dropped objects, opening doors, retrieving the phone, helping with transfers or anything else their partner may need. They can be trained to do a number of things. Trained to alert their handler to dangerous or potentially deadly blood sugar highs and lows. Many dogs are trained to call 911 on a special K-9 Alert Phone if their partner cannot be roused. Handler may show signs of visible disability, but likely will not. Could be any age from very, very young to a senior citizen. Trained to respond to their handler's seizures. The dog may retrieve medication, utilize deep pressure stimulation to end a seizure early, fetch a nearby person to help or call 911. Other trained tasks are common as well. Handler may or may not show signs of physical disability.
